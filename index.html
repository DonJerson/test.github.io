Statics & Mechanics of Materials

Hollow Shaft Properties

The Polar Moment of Inertia (J) measures a shaft's resistance to twisting. For a hollow shaft, it's calculated based on the outer (OD) and inner (ID) diameters.

J = (π / 32) * (OD⁴ - ID⁴)

Composite Beams

When two materials are joined to form a beam, we use the Transformed Section Method to analyze it. This involves transforming the stiffer material into an equivalent area of the less stiff material using the modular ratio `n = E_stiff / E_weak`.

The steps are:

  1. Calculate the modular ratio, `n`.
  2. Create the transformed section by multiplying the width of the stiffer material by `n`.
  3. Find the Neutral Axis (centroid) of the new, single-material shape.
  4. Calculate the Moment of Inertia (`I_NA`) of the transformed section about the neutral axis.
  5. Determine the maximum allowable moment by checking the stress limits in each material.

Structural Dynamics

Dynamics is the study of how systems move under time-varying forces. The concepts below form the foundation of analyzing vibrations in structures.

A simple pendulum is a fundamental model for understanding vibration. Its natural frequency (how fast it "wants" to swing) depends only on its length and gravity.

Angular Frequency (ω):

ω = √(g / L)

Natural Frequency (f) in Hertz:

f = ω / (2π) = (1 / 2π) * √(g / L)

This formula relies on the **small-angle approximation**, where for small swings, the motion is considered Simple Harmonic Motion.

The motion of a standard single-degree-of-freedom system (like a mass on a spring with a shock absorber) is described by this second-order linear differential equation:

mẍ + cẋ + kx = F

The symbols with dots represent time derivatives:

  • x: Displacement from the equilibrium position.
  • ẋ: Velocity (dx/dt), the rate of change of displacement.
  • ẍ: Acceleration (d²x/dt²), the rate of change of velocity.

This equation is often normalized into a standard form using these key parameters:

  • Natural Frequency (ωₙ): `ωₙ = √(k/m)`. The frequency the system would oscillate at with no damping.
  • Damping Ratio (ζ): `ζ = c / (2√(km))`. A dimensionless number that describes how quickly oscillations die out.
    • `ζ < 1`: Underdamped (oscillates to a stop)
    • `ζ = 1`: Critically Damped (fastest return to zero with no oscillation)
    • `ζ > 1`: Overdamped (slow, sluggish return to zero)

For complex structures like a multi-story building, the simple stiffness `k` becomes a **stiffness matrix [K]**. This matrix relates the forces applied at each floor to the displacements of all floors.

{F} = [K]{u}

The physical meaning of an element `Kᵢⱼ` is: "The force felt at Floor `i` when Floor `j` is displaced by one unit, while all other floors are held still."

For a 5-story "shear building", the 5x5 stiffness matrix has a specific, banded structure based on the stiffness of each story (`k₁` to `k₅`):

[K] = 
[ k₁+k₂   -k₂      0       0       0   ]
[  -k₂   k₂+k₃    -k₃      0       0   ]
[   0     -k₃    k₃+k₄    -k₄      0   ]
[   0      0      -k₄    k₄+k₅    -k₅  ]
[   0      0       0      -k₅     k₅   ]

A "vibration mode" consists of a **natural frequency** and its corresponding **mode shape**. Modal analysis is the process of finding these modes for a structure.

The process starts with the equation of free vibration (no damping, no external force):

[M]{ü} + [K]{u} = {0}

By assuming the solution is harmonic (sinusoidal), this differential equation is transformed into an algebraic **Eigenvalue Problem**:

([K] - ω²[M]){φ} = {0}

Solving this problem yields:

  • The **Eigenvalues:** These are the squares of the natural frequencies (`ω₁²`, `ω₂²`, ...).
  • The **Eigenvectors (`{φ}`):** These are the mode shapes, which describe the pattern of deformation for each corresponding frequency.

Advanced & Foundational Topics

Column Buckling

Slender columns under compression can fail not by crushing, but by suddenly bending sideways. This is called **buckling**. The theoretical maximum load a column can support before buckling is given by **Euler's Critical Load** formula.

The formula can be expressed using the column's material properties (`E`), its cross-sectional area (`A`), and its **slenderness ratio (`KL/r`)**.

P_cr = (π² * E * A) / (KL/r)²

It's crucial to note that this formula applies to long, slender columns. Short, stocky columns will fail by crushing (yielding) at a load lower than their theoretical buckling load.

Determinants & Solving Linear Systems

In structural analysis, we often need to solve the system of linear equations `[A]{x} = {b}` (where `A` is the stiffness matrix `[K]`). The **determinant** of matrix `[A]` is a single number that tells us about the nature of the solution.

  • If **`det(A) ≠ 0`**: The system has **exactly one unique solution**. The matrix is invertible, and the structure is stable.
  • If **`det(A) = 0`**: The system does **not** have a unique solution. It will have either **no solution** or **infinitely many solutions**. In structural terms, this often means the structure is unstable (i.e., it's a mechanism that can move without any applied force).

Nonlinear Analysis

While the equation `[K]{u} = {F}` is linear, real-world structures can behave nonlinearly. This occurs when:

  • Geometric Nonlinearity: The structure undergoes large deflections, which changes its stiffness.
  • Material Nonlinearity: The material itself yields or cracks, changing its properties.

Solving these problems requires a **nonlinear solution process**, where the load is applied in small steps, and the stiffness matrix `[K]` is updated at each step to reflect the changing state of the structure.
